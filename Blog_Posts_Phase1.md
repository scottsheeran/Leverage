**Target Keywords:**

- "ATS keywords" (1,200 searches)
- "what does ATS look for" (890 searches)

---

## OUTLINE

### Hook (100 words)
- You're applying to jobs. You seem qualified. But silence.
- 75% of resumes never reach a human because of Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS).
- Most people think it's their resume. It's not. It's the system.
- Here's what actually gets through the ATS, and how to make sure your resume does too.

### Section 1: What is an ATS and Why It Rejects You (250 words)
- ATS = software that reads resumes, filters by keywords
- Most companies use it (doesn't matter if you're applying to Fortune 500 or startup)
- It's looking for keyword matches, not "good writing"
- The problem: generic resume language doesn't match job description language
- Example: You write "managed teams" but the job says "team leadership" — ATS doesn't see it as a match

### Section 2: The 5 Most Important Keywords (400 words)
For each category, give 3-5 real examples:

1. **Job Title Keywords**
   - Don't be cute. If the job is "Product Manager," use "Product Manager" somewhere in your resume
   - Examples: If you were a project lead, call it "Project Lead" not "Orchestrator of Cross-functional Teams"
   
2. **Technical Skills**
   - List exact software/languages they mention
   - If job mentions "SQL," list SQL (not "database querying")
   - Tool tip: Use CTRL+F on the job description to find all technical terms

3. **Industry/Domain Keywords**
   - What's the industry? "SaaS," "fintech," "healthcare"?
   - Use that exact language
   - Example: If it's fintech, say "fintech" somewhere, not "financial technology sector"

4. **Responsibility Keywords**
   - Look for verbs: "owns," "drives," "scales," "grows"
   - Match your bullet points to their language
   - Example: If job says "scales infrastructure," use "scaled" in your resume

5. **Company Values/Keywords**
   - Read their "About" section
   - Do they emphasize "innovation," "customer-centric," "agile"?
   - Work those words into your experience

### Section 3: The Practical Process (300 words)
Step-by-step how to find and add keywords:

1. Copy the job description
2. Use CTRL+F to search for key terms (technology, job titles, responsibilities)
3. Cross-reference with your resume
4. Rewrite bullet points to include missing keywords naturally
5. Don't keyword stuff — 1-2 mentions per keyword is enough
6. Tool: Use LVRGE ATS Optimizer to check your match score

### Section 4: The Human Part (200 words)
- Adding keywords matters, but recruiters also read resumes
- Don't sacrifice readability for keywords
- Still tell a story: "Scaled AWS infrastructure that reduced latency by 40%" (keyword + result)
- The goal: beat the ATS *and* impress the human

- Link to app

# BLOG POST #2: June 8, 2026
## Title (SEO + Conversion)
"Why Your Generic Cover Letter Gets Rejected (And What Hiring Managers Actually Want in 60 Seconds)"

**URL Slug:** `why-generic-cover-letters-fail`

**Meta Description:** "Hiring managers spend 6 seconds on your cover letter. We analyzed 50+ applications to find what actually works (and what immediately gets deleted)."

**Target Audience:** Job seekers frustrated with cover letter writing

**Target Keywords:**
- "how to write a cover letter" (8,100 monthly searches)
- "cover letter tips" (3,600 searches)
- "why cover letters matter" (1,200 searches)

---

## OUTLINE

### Hook (100 words)
- You spent 2 hours writing a thoughtful cover letter
- Hiring manager spends 6 seconds reading it
- Then deletes it
- The problem isn't effort. It's that your cover letter sounds like every other candidate's.
- Here's what actually works (and what immediately gets you rejected).

### Section 1: The Problem With Generic Cover Letters (250 words)
- "I am writing to express my interest in..."
- Hiring managers see this 100+ times a week
- AI-generated letters are even more obvious
- The issue: You're trying to be professional. Hiring managers want to be interested.
- Real stat: "Dear Hiring Manager" cover letters have lower response rates than personalized ones

### Section 2: What Hiring Managers Actually Want (350 words)
The 4 things that make them STOP and read:

1. **Specificity**
   - Not: "I'm excited about your company"
   - Yes: "I saw you launched Feature X last month — it solves problem Y better than competitors because Z"
   - Proof: You actually researched them

2. **Show You Know the Role**
   - Reference specific responsibilities from job description
   - "Your job description mentions scaling systems — I did this at [previous company]"
   - You're not generic, you're targeted

3. **One Specific Win**
   - Not: "I'm a hard worker"
   - Yes: "When I led X project, we saw Y result (metric)"
   - One proof point > ten generic claims

4. **Your Voice (Not a Robot)**
   - Let personality show
   - Use "I," not "the applicant"
   - A slight conversational tone beats corporate language
   - Example: "I'm not a perfect fit yet, but I learn fast and I'm hungry for this role"

### Section 3: The Formula That Works (300 words)
Give them a replicable template:

**Paragraph 1 (Hook):** 1-2 sentences
- Why this role? Why this company?
- Must be specific (not generic)

**Paragraph 2 (Proof):** 2-3 sentences
- Give one concrete example of relevant success
- Include a metric if possible

**Paragraph 3 (Bridge):** 1-2 sentences
- How does your experience map to their needs?
- Show you understand their problem

**Paragraph 4 (Close):** 1-2 sentences
- Call to action (polite, confident)
- "I'd love to discuss how my experience with X can help you with Y"

### Section 4: The Tone Matters (200 words)
- Hiring managers want to imagine working with you
- Tone variations by company culture:
  - Fortune 500: More formal, but still personal
  - Startup: Conversational, show personality
  - Tech: Slightly irreverent is okay
  - Non-tech: Play it straight
- Use LVRGE to generate in different tones (direct, warm, formal, bold) and pick what fits

### CTA (50 words)
- "Writing a custom cover letter for every job is exhausting. LVRGE generates tailored cover letters in 4 different tones in 30 seconds. Free to try — paste job description, pick your tone, done."
- Link to app

**Post Length:** ~1,200-1,400 words
**Time to Write:** 2-3 hours

# BLOG POST #3: June 15, 2026
## Title (SEO + Conversion)
"Track Your Job Search Like a Business (Stop Applying to the Same Company Twice)"

**URL Slug:** `job-search-tracking-spreadsheet`

**Meta Description:** "Apply to 50+ jobs and forget where you sent them? Use this spreadsheet + checklist to track every application, follow-up, and outcome."

**Target Audience:** Active job seekers applying to multiple jobs

**Target Keywords:**
- "job search spreadsheet" (1,800 monthly searches)
- "how to track job applications" (2,100 searches)
- "job application tracker" (1,500 searches)

---

## OUTLINE

### Hook (100 words)
- You've applied to... how many jobs? 10? 30? 50?
- Do you remember which ones? Their names?
- Have you accidentally applied to the same company twice?
- Most job seekers wing it. Then wonder why they can't follow up effectively.
- Here's how to actually track your job search like a business.

### Section 1: Why Tracking Matters (250 words)
- You're running a mini sales pipeline (you're the product)
- No follow-up = lost opportunities
- Duplicate applications = look disorganized
- What to track:
  - Where did you apply?
  - When?
  - What role?
  - What's the status?
  - How does it match your skills?

### Section 2: The Simple Tracking System (300 words)
Core columns for a job search tracker:

1. **Company**
2. **Job Title**
3. **Date Applied**
4. **Link to Application** (or job posting URL)
5. **Status** (Applied / Interview / Rejected / Offer)
6. **Fit Score** (1-10, based on how well it matches your skills)
7. **Follow-up Date** (when to check in)
8. **Notes** (what they asked, your impressions, etc.)

**Why each column matters:**
- Company + Title: Quick reference (no duplicate applications)
- Date Applied: Know your pipeline age (old applications need follow-ups)
- Link: Go back to application details quickly
- Status: Prioritize follow-ups on "Applied" jobs
- Fit Score: Organize by likelihood (focus on 8-10s)
- Follow-up Date: Know when to send a "checking in" email
- Notes: Remember what they asked, what you said, red flags

### Section 3: Tracking Behavior (300 words)
**Weekly review process:**
- Every Friday, review applications from 2 weeks ago
- Identify "Applied" status jobs that haven't moved
- Send 2-3 polite follow-up emails ("Wanted to check in on my application for...")
- Update status based on responses
- Flag interviews and prep notes there

**Monthly review:**
- How many applications? (Should be 15-25+/month in active search)
- Conversion to interviews? (Should be 5-10%)
- Upcoming interviews? (Prep notes)
- Rejections? (Learn from feedback if given)

**3-Month review:**
- What types of companies respond best? (Double down)
- What keywords in job descriptions match your wins? (Use in next resume)
- Is your strategy working? (Adjust if <5% interview conversion)

- Google Sheets (free, simple)
- Airtable (free, more powerful)
- LVRGE Job Tracker (integrated with resume/cover letter tools)
- Notion (if you're already using it for life management)
